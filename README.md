## 📖 About the Project

**ShopSphere** is a US-based e-commerce company selling popular consumer electronics and accessories. Founded in 2018, ShopSphere has grown steadily but now faces increasing competition from peer companies, along with new challenges and opportunities in a shifting market.

Between 2021–2022, ShopSphere's book of business grew to nearly **3,998 customers** and **5,885 transactions**, generating **over $1 billion** in transaction revenue. This project dives into that data to answer key questions from the Marketing and Finance teams, evaluating ShopSphere's performance across two years and translating raw numbers into insights that cross-functional teams can act on.

The analysis focuses on three pillars:

| 📈 Sales Trends | 📦 Product Performance | 👥 Customer Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue, order volume, and Average Order Value (AOV) | Product lines, payment methods, and transaction status | Segmentation and engagement patterns to boost retention |

## 🎯 Executive Summary

### 1. Revenue Growth & Peak Performance
- **2022 was the strongest year**, with fluctuating transaction performance driven by high demand in April, July, and October.
- **Q2 2022** saw the highest revenue of the entire period — **$1.29B in May 2022** — the best-performing month overall.
- **Dec 2021 – Mar 2022** ($420M profit) also held strong, following peak sales in **November 2021** ($420M profit).

### 2. A Volatile 2022
- A sales anomaly and sharp fluctuation hit in 2022, especially across Q2–Q4, with **April ($216M), July ($202M), and September ($54M)** marking the highest-profit months.
- The **Q3–Q4 revenue decline** suggests a major downturn — likely tied to external market conditions, softer consumer demand, or internal operational shifts.
- The **Q4 profit spike** aligns with a possible new product launch, based on **1,024 units** of Mobile & Tablets ordered.
- In **April**, a large gap opened between profit and revenue — largely driven by Women's Fashion, which accounted for 172 of 526 total units sold.
- In **July**, sales were more evenly spread across Soghat, Mobile & Tablets, and Superstar categories.

### 3. Key Takeaways
- 🔍 Investigate the drivers behind the 2022 fluctuation (market shifts, new product lines, internal changes).
- 🚀 Leverage historically strong periods (e.g., Q4 in both years) to sharpen marketing and sales strategy.
- 🔁 Reassess the 2023 strategy — pricing, promotions, discounting, and customer engagement — to regain momentum.

## 🔎 Insight Deep-Dive

### Sales Trend

**Sales Profit**

**① High Fluctuation (Q2–Q4 2022) — A Major Sales Anomaly**
- Q2 (Apr–Jun) was the strongest quarter at **$333M**, though heavily carried by April; by June, sales had normalized.
- Oct–Nov 2022 profit fell to **$33M** (vs. $188M the year prior) — an **80–85% drop**. Notably, Oct–Nov was the *best* period in 2021 but the *worst* in 2022.

**② Declining Growth Through 2022 — Weak Consumer Demand**
- Every quarter in 2022 showed a fluctuating trend.
- **Q1:** Started strong — $63M in January, ~400% higher than 2021.
- **Q2–Q3:** Big spikes in April, July, and September, but sales consistently reverted to baseline afterward — pointing to product-specific demand issues. ShopSphere may need to renegotiate supplier deals to lower costs and secure more exclusive pricing.
- **Q4:** The weakest quarter of 2022 — $14M in October, declining further to $12M in November.

### Average Order Value

**① Stable at a Low Baseline (Jan 2021 – mid 2022)**
- April 2022 posted the highest AOV of the period, peaking at **$1.1M**; April 2021 was the lowest, at just **$425K**.

**② 2022 AOV Is Higher — and More Volatile**
- Excluding the September anomaly, 2022's AOV (**$861K**) outperforms 2021's (**$760K**).
- September alone pushed 2022's AOV to **$1.09M** — but the sharp collapse afterward signals risk, not a genuine win.

**③ October 2022 AOV Decline Signals an Anomaly**
- AOV fell from a September peak of **~$7.6M** to **~$795K** in October — an **89% drop**.
- August's AOV (~$886K) suggests that, absent the October anomaly, AOV growth would look far more stable. No lasting "new normal" was formed — the spike wasn't scalable or retained.

### Order Count

- From mid-2021 to mid-2022, unit volume trended clearly upward, with each peak higher than the last — signaling demand growth and an aggressive sales push, despite sharp drops after every rise.
- **Total orders grew 46%** from 2021 to 2022.
- Sales began declining gradually from April 2022, interrupted by the September 2022 anomaly.
- **Revenue is volume-driven, but profit is event-driven** — high-sales periods don't consistently translate into profit, pointing to pricing and cost inefficiencies.

### Product Category Performance

**The Best and Worst**
- **Mobiles & Tablets** underperformed for most of the period but surged in **September 2022** to become the #1 category, totaling **$1.3B** in revenue — including **$528M** in September alone (~50% of total revenue that month). Strong in total revenue, but weak in long-term consistency.
- **Appliances** ranked as the second/third best-performing category (~$538–601M total) and was the most *balanced* in terms of sales and quantity — arguably the best category overall if not for the September anomaly.
- **Books** lagged far behind all other categories, totaling just **$17M**.
- **Women's Fashion** posted a surprise profit spike in April 2022 (**$143.97M**), driven by 32.7% of customers purchasing from this category that month.

**AOV Over Time**
- **Mobiles & Tablets** ($5.15M), **Entertainment** ($2.78M), and **Appliances** ($2.45M) are the largest contributors to AOV.
- **Superstore** has low price points and stays stable, but doesn't move the needle much on AOV (**$268.74K**).

**Brand Performance**

*(Scoped to the five brands the Marketing team asked about: Samsung, Apple, Huawei, Lenovo, and Sony — all other products excluded.)*

- **Samsung** delivered consistently strong sales year-over-year, totaling **$588.76M** — the highest-revenue brand overall.
- **Apple** ($444.86M) and **Lenovo** ($78.34M) round out the second and third spots.
- **Apple** ($6.45M), **Samsung** ($4.72M), and **Huawei** ($3.95M) contribute the most to AOV.

## 👥 Customer Behavior

Customers are split into two registration paths — **1,984 "Normal"** customers (register first, then order) and **880 "Pre-Register"** customers (order first, then register). Segmentation is built on intrinsic purchasing behavior, with brand and category attributes layered on afterward to interpret and activate each segment.

> Revenue growth is driven by episodic, high-value events rather than sustained customer activity. Loyal "Normal" customers are declining, while "Pre-Register" customers show high potential but inconsistent engagement — strong upside, weak retention fundamentals.

**Normal Customers**
- The **Loyal / High-Value** segment ramps up from mid-2021, peaking around September 2021 and January 2022 with **$716.79M** in total revenue. Units and revenue move in lockstep — but both collapse toward near-zero after mid-2022.
- The **Low-Engagement** segment starts nearly flat in early 2021, then grows sharply mid-2021 to **$27.88M** in revenue, with several spiky peaks where revenue growth outpaces unit growth — evidence that these purchases are campaign-driven, not habitual.

**Pre-Register Customers**
- The **Extreme High-Volume** segment shows one sharp spike in **April 2022** ($237.13M in revenue) — likely a single bulk order, special event, or major customer.
- The **Loyal / High-Value** segment stays nearly flat for a long stretch, then posts one massive revenue spike ($570.78M) with minimal unit increase.
- The **Low-Engagement** segment shows that pre-registration alone doesn't guarantee activation — behavior closely mirrors low-engagement Normal customers.

**Bottom line:** Normal customers drive most transaction *volume* through repeated, low-engagement purchases — providing operational stability. Pre-registered customers, while fewer in number, are more likely to generate high-impact, bulk-style purchases. This creates a stability-vs-upside trade-off that calls for distinct engagement strategies per segment.

## 💡 Recommendations

**1. Reduce reliance on anomaly-driven revenue**
2022's revenue leaned heavily on one-off events (e.g., the September Mobile & Tablets spike), which aren't sustainable. Prioritize consistently strong categories like **Appliances**, which show balanced revenue and unit sales, and reduce dependence on short-term surges.

**2. Refine pricing, cost control, and promotions**
Revenue is volume-driven while profit is event-driven — a clear sign of pricing and cost inefficiencies. Renegotiate supplier pricing, rein in aggressive discounting during high-volume periods, and shift promotions to protect margin rather than just chase order count.

**3. Rebuild retention with segment-specific strategies**
Loyal "Normal" customers declined sharply after mid-2022, while "Pre-Register" customers showed high value but inconsistent engagement. Roll out loyalty incentives for repeat Normal customers and targeted activation campaigns for high-potential Pre-Register customers.

**4. Leverage top brands for bundling and upsell**
Samsung and Apple consistently drive the highest revenue and AOV. Use them as anchor products for bundles, cross-sells, and premium offers to lift AOV in lower-impact categories like Superstore and Books.
